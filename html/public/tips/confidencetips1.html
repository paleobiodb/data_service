1. Strauss and Sadler (1989)

Summary: Observed stratigraphic ranges almost always underestimate true longevities. This classic method calculates confidence intervals on the endpoints of local stratigraphic ranges using a Bayesian approach. This method can also be applied to composite sections; confidence intervals may be placed on times of origin (first occurrence) and extinction (last occurrence) for entire species or lineages.

Assumptions:
  		• Fossil horizons are randomly distributed throughout stratigraphic ranges.
  		• Collection intensity has been uniform over the stratigraphic range.
  		• There is no significant correlation between gap size and stratigraphic position (see Tranpostion Test).

    Output: Confidence intervals are given graphically and in a table for the selected confidence level.

    An example of the graphical output displayed by the Strauss and Sadler (1989) method. The hollow bars represent the known range of fossil horizons (in this case showing the point of first occurrence or origination) and the solid lines demark the interval within which, based on the data in the Paleodatabase, we are confident that the real point of origination was likely to have occurred.

    References:
  		• Marshall, C. R. (1990) Confidence intervals on stratigraphic ranges. Paleobiology 16(1):1-10.
  		• Strauss, D. and Sadler, P. M. (1989) Classical confidence intervals and Bayesian probability estimates for ends of local taxon ranges. Mathematical Geology 21:411-427.

    2. Marshall (1994)

    Summary: The equations for calculating classical confidence intervals on the end points of stratigraphic ranges are based on the restrictive assumption of randomly distributed fossil finds. This method can be used for calculating confidence intervals on the end-points of stratigraphic ranges with partial relaxation of this assumption: the method will work for any continuous distribution of gap sizes, not just those generated by random processes. The price paid for the generality of the approach are two-fold: (1) there are uncertainties associated with the sizes of confidence intervals, and (2) for large confidence values (e.g., 95%) a rich fossil record is required to place upper bounds on the corresponding confidence intervals.

    Assumptions:
  		• Fossil horizons need not be randomly distributed throughout stratigraphic ranges.
  		• There is no significant correlation between gap size and stratigraphic position (see Tranposition Test).
  		• The uncertainty on confidence intervals with user-selcted confidence level will be calculated at the 0.05 level.

    Output:

    Example of the confidence intervals displayed by the Marshall (1994) method. The hollow bars represent the known range of fossil horizons (in this case showing the point of first occurrence or origination) and the solid lines (figure left) demark the interval within which, based on the data in the Paleodatabase, we are confident that the real point of origination was likely to have occurred. The arrow graphic (figure right) demarks the lower uncertainty bound for the confidence interval and indicates that the upper bound can not be calculted with the given information.

    References:
  		• Marshall, C. R. (1994) Confidence intervals on stratigraphic ranges: partial relaxation of the assumption of randomly distributed fossil horizons. Paleobiology 20(4):459-469.

  		• Locations of finds of different taxa represent realizations of independent, stationary Poisson processes.

    Example of the confidence intervals displayed by the Solow (1996) method.     Transposition Test

    Summary:

    Many confidence interval methods are based on the assumption that there is no significant correlation between gap size and stratigraphic position. Marshall (1994) suggests using the transposition test (Kreyszig 1979: p. 932) to test for the presence of an increasing or decreasing trend in gap size with stratigraphic position. The tranposition test is automatically run and outputted for all the above confidence interval methods, regardless of their explicit assumptions.

    Output: An output of "1" indicates that a significant correlation exists between gap size and stratigraphic position for a given taxon, whereas an output of "0" indicates no sginificant relationship.

    References:
  		• Kreyszig, E. (1979) Advanced engineering mathematics. Wiley and Sons, New York.
  		• Marshall, C. R. (1994) Confidence intervals on stratigraphic ranges: partial relaxation of the assumption of randomly distributed fossil horizons. Paleobiology 20(4):459-469.
